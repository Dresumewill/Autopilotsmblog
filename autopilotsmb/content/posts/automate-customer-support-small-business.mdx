The support ticket that arrives at 2am. The fifth customer this week asking the same refund question. The onboarding email chain that takes 45 minutes every time.

If any of that sounds familiar, this post is for you.

Customer support automation isn't about replacing humans — it's about making sure humans only have to handle the things that actually require humans.

Here's the system that works for small businesses.

## The 70/30 Framework

The goal is simple: **automate 70% of support interactions** so your team can focus on the 30% that actually need a person.

That 70% typically includes:
- "Where's my order?" questions
- Password resets and account access
- FAQ-style policy questions (returns, shipping, pricing)
- Initial intake and triage
- After-hours acknowledgment

The 30% that needs humans:
- Complex complaints and escalations
- Sales conversations
- Custom requests
- Anything emotionally charged

## Step 1: Audit Your Current Support Volume

Before automating anything, pull your last 3 months of support tickets and categorize them. You'll likely find that 5–8 question types make up the bulk of your volume.

**Tools for this:**
- Export from Zendesk, Freshdesk, or Help Scout
- Use Claude to categorize and summarize patterns from the raw export

You're looking for your "Top 10 Most Asked Questions" — this is what you'll automate first.

## Step 2: Build Your Knowledge Base

AI chatbots are only as good as what they know. Before you deploy anything, create a structured knowledge base:

```
knowledge-base/
├── products/
│   ├── features.md
│   └── pricing.md
├── policies/
│   ├── returns.md
│   ├── shipping.md
│   └── privacy.md
├── account/
│   ├── reset-password.md
│   └── cancel-subscription.md
└── FAQ.md
```

Keep each document focused and factual. Use bullet points over paragraphs — AI tools parse structured content better.

**Pro tip**: Write your policies in a conversational Q&A format. Instead of "Returns are accepted within 30 days," write "Can I return my order? Yes — we accept returns within 30 days of purchase."

## Step 3: Choose Your Automation Stack

For most small businesses, the right stack is:

### Tier 1: Chat Widget (Front-line)
**Tidio with Lyro AI** (best for SMBs) or **Intercom Fin** (better for SaaS)

This handles:
- Instant responses to FAQ questions
- Lead capture when no agents available
- After-hours coverage

### Tier 2: Automation Platform (Back-end)
**Make** or **Zapier**

This handles:
- Routing tickets to the right queue
- Triggering email sequences based on support issue type
- Escalation notifications to Slack

### Tier 3: Email Sequences (Follow-up)
**ConvertKit** or **ActiveCampaign**

This handles:
- Automated follow-ups after ticket resolution
- NPS surveys
- Onboarding drips triggered by support events

## Step 4: Set Up Your First Automation

Here's a complete automation to start with — the **order status inquiry workflow**.

**Trigger**: Customer sends message containing "where is my order" or "order status" or "tracking"

**Flow**:
1. AI chatbot responds instantly with: "I can help with that! Could you share your order number?"
2. Customer provides order number
3. Bot queries your order system via API (or Zapier lookup)
4. Bot returns: tracking link + estimated delivery + carrier name
5. Asks: "Is there anything else I can help with?"

**Result**: Zero human involvement. Customer happy in under 60 seconds.

## Step 5: Define Your Escalation Rules

Automation without clear escalation rules creates frustrated customers. Define exactly when the bot should hand off to a human.

**Always escalate when:**
- Customer uses words like "angry," "furious," "scam," "refund," "lawyer"
- The bot can't answer after 2 attempts
- Issue involves a dollar amount above your threshold (e.g., >$200)
- Customer explicitly asks for a human

**Escalation flow:**
1. Bot acknowledges: "I want to make sure you get the right help on this. Let me connect you with a team member."
2. Create ticket tagged "ESCALATION" in your help desk
3. Ping your team in Slack with context summary
4. Auto-reply to customer with expected response time

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Mistake 1: Automating before you understand your customers**
Don't build a chatbot before you know what questions your customers actually ask. Audit first, automate second.

**Mistake 2: Making the bot too hard to escape**
If a customer has to click through 8 options to reach a human, they'll leave and post a bad review. Make "Talk to a person" always one click away.

**Mistake 3: Forgetting to update the knowledge base**
Your products, policies, and pricing change. Your AI knowledge base needs to change too. Schedule a monthly review.

**Mistake 4: Letting the bot handle emotional situations**
A customer who just lost a project because your tool failed doesn't want a chatbot. Train your automation to detect frustration signals and escalate immediately.

## Measuring Success

Track these metrics monthly:

| Metric | Target |
|--------|--------|
| Bot resolution rate | >60% |
| First response time | <2 minutes |
| CSAT score | >4.2/5.0 |
| Escalation rate | <25% |
| Tickets requiring human | Decreasing MoM |
